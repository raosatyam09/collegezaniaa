UGC INITIATIVES TO REVOLUTIONIZE IN INDIA?

With the current public discourse over the University Grants Commission’s (UGC) new initiatives to revolutionize the higher education system in India, the key question is: Do we stick to the Western model that originally influenced our universities, build a framework based on our own traditional learning processes, or establish a hybrid model combining the best of both to address local and international needs? As is well understood, Western higher education models focus heavily on research that adds to the knowledge of society. This model encourages cooperation among socially oriented agencies and university departments in collaboration to solve problems, spur innovation, and create new knowledge. Indian universities, with the enormous numbers of students pursuing degrees mainly as a means to guarantee a job, tend to put research in the backseat. While the UGC has brought in a number of regulations to make PhD programmes more streamlined, developing a robust research culture is still a relatively new task for us.

Considering the UGC’s interest in improving the standards of higher education in our nation, it would be best if it took suggestions from experts in the field. Some of the important observations are as follows: Safeguarding academic freedom is essential to maintain transparency and promote development. Transparency entails that the appointments of vice chancellors and registrars should be free from any monetary influence; these roles should be given purely on the basis of academic success, administrative experience, and the vision the candidates can contribute to their potential roles. If appointments are based on money, the very concept of “quality” in higher education loses all meaning. One of the common problems in our higher education system is political intervention at local, state, and central levels in key areas like faculty recruitment, syllabus formulation, and research grants. These are severely weakening the system. Frequently, the ruling party prescribes what can and cannot be taught. Equally, the research topics get funded in preference by those who hold power over their desired preferences, instead of providing the principal investigators with the intellectual autonomy to research areas of their own interest. Equally, we constantly hear about the impact of money and influence on the recruitment and promotions of the faculty. Without tackling these long-standing problems, higher education will forever remain a far-fetched dream of becoming excellent. The most important element that must be confronted in our higher education system is quality, not the undue focus we tend to give to quantity. Higher education should be for those with a legitimate academic interest in acquiring knowledge. Academic degrees should not merely be a job requirement. What we learn and what we actually do professionally often bear little relationship. More often than not, we develop industry specific skills based on hands-on experience upon employment and not upon what we know from schools and colleges.

Our education system largely fails in bridging the gap between classroom learning and real-world application. One of the biggest mistakes we, as a society, continue to make is equating academic degrees with job eligibility. This mindset has significantly restricted the true purpose of higher education — pursuing research. Consequently, research is often seen as just another degree to be earned, rather than as a rigorous intellectual pursuit aimed at generating new knowledge. Quantifying a research degree as an essential qualification for the selection of teaching positions has hugely watered down the integrity of our research programmes. Real research is an inductive and discretionary activity that demands full-time devotion, strict compliance with stringent guidelines, and, finally, the creation of new understanding and knowledge in the selected area. Last but not least, in order to improve India’s higher education system, the UGC should contemplate adopting a number of key steps. These involve implementing research based evaluations, promoting interdisciplinary learning and critical thinking, and minimizing use of contract faculty through investments in permanent recruitment and development. Encouraging research on Indian literature and philosophy, enhancing community outreach, and maintaining a balance between work and life for faculty and students are also crucial. Compulsory counselling facilities need to be initiated to aid student welfare, while universities need to work with policymakers to combat issues of healthcare, education, and climate change. Providing more autonomy to students to choose courses, incorporating new areas such as Artificial Intelligence and promoting student start-ups with university facilitation are also important. All these should be an integral part of the UGC’s strategic vision to revolutionize higher education in India.
